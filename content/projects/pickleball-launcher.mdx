## Overview

Commercial pickleball launchers sell for $150–400. Mechanically, they are two DC
motors spinning rubber wheels in opposite directions with a ball fed between
them. That gap between what the machine *is* and what it *costs* is an
engineering opportunity — so this project sets a hard constraint: **a working
launcher for under $100**, designed and documented like a real product, not a
weekend hack.

The project has two objectives. First, it's a complete mechanical engineering
exercise at small scale — requirements, component sizing, cost management,
build, and a test campaign that checks predictions against reality. Second, if
v1 holds up, the ~$95 bill of materials against a $175–200 selling price is a
real margin on a real product.

## Problem Statement

Design and build a machine that can repeatedly launch a standard pickleball
(74 mm diameter, ~24 g) at controllable speed, powered from a portable 12V
source, with total component cost under $100.

## Design Constraints

| Constraint | Value | Driver |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Total BOM cost | < $100 | Project requirement — forces real engineering tradeoffs |
| Ball compatibility | 74 mm pickleball | Standard equipment, non-negotiable |
| Power source | 12V battery, portable | Courts don't have outlets |
| Speed control | Continuously variable | A launcher with one speed is a novelty, not a training tool |
| Fabrication | Hand tools, scrap lumber | No machine shop access — design must respect that |

## Working Principle

Two rubber wheels counter-rotate with a gap slightly smaller than the ball.
The ball drops in, both wheel surfaces grip it, and it accelerates through the
contact zone to (ideally) the wheel surface speed. Spin is controlled by
running the wheels at different speeds — equal speeds launch flat, a
differential adds topspin or backspin.

**Contact geometry.** With 76 mm drive wheels and a 74 mm ball, the wheel gap
is set to 65–68 mm — compressing the ball by 6–9 mm. Too little squeeze and
the wheels slip instead of gripping; too much and the ball binds and stalls
the motors. This one dimension carries most of the design risk, so the motor
mounts are slotted to make the gap tunable during testing.

**Launch velocity.** The ceiling on launch speed is wheel surface speed:

```text
v_surface = ω · r = (RPM · 2π / 60) · 0.038 m

775 motor, no load @ 12V:  ~10,000–13,000 RPM  →  ~40–52 m/s surface speed
Under load (realistic):     a fraction of that — this is what testing measures
```

Real launch velocity will land well below the no-load ceiling because of motor
sag under load, slip in the contact zone, and energy spent compressing the
ball. Quantifying that gap — predicted vs. measured — is the point of the
test campaign.

## Component Selection

Every part is a cost-vs-capability decision documented against the $100 cap:

| Subsystem | Selection | Cost | Rationale |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Drive motors (×2) | 775-class 12V DC | $33.98 | Best torque-per-dollar in hobby DC motors; ubiquitous mounting pattern |
| Motor drivers | BTS7960 43A H-bridge (×2) | $15.38 | Massively over-rated for the load — headroom instead of magic smoke |
| Drive wheels | 76 mm / 82A inline skate wheels | $13.99 | Correct diameter and durometer for grip; $3.50/wheel vs. $20+ for "launcher wheels" |
| Battery | 12V 4Ah sealed lead-acid | $16.79 | Cheapest portable 12V that handles motor inrush; weight is acceptable on a cart |
| Charger | 12V 1.3A smart SLA charger | $6.69 | Set-and-forget charging |
| Frame | Scrap 2×4 lumber | ~$0 | Stiff, workable with hand tools, free |
| Hardware | M4/M5 fasteners, mounts | ~$8 | — |

**Running total: ~$95.** The discipline here isn't finding cheap parts — it's
knowing which specs matter (wheel durometer, driver current headroom) and
which don't (frame material, aesthetics) at this stage.

## Engineering Process

1. **Requirements first.** Speed range, portability, and cost were fixed before any part was picked.
2. **Physics before parts.** Wheel-speed-to-launch-velocity math sized the motors; contact mechanics sized the wheel gap.
3. **Design for tuning.** The highest-uncertainty parameter (wheel gap) gets slotted mounts, not a fixed dimension — because the first analysis is always somewhat wrong, and good designs admit it.
4. **Test against prediction.** The build phase ends with measured range and velocity data plotted against the projectile-motion model.

## Test Campaign (planned)

- Launch velocity measurement via slow-motion video against a distance reference
- Range vs. motor PWM duty cycle, mapped across the throttle range
- Predicted vs. actual trajectory using standard projectile motion with a drag estimate
- Repeatability: shot-to-shot dispersion at fixed settings — the number that decides whether this is a *product* or a *prototype*

## Status & Next Steps

Design phase is complete and the BOM is finalized. Current phase: procurement
and frame build. After v1 testing, planned iterations include a ball hopper
and feed mechanism, elevation adjustment, and a microcontroller-based control
upgrade (replacing manual PWM with programmable shot sequences).

## Lessons So Far

Design-to-cost is a different sport than design-to-performance. A $400
launcher is easy. Deleting 75% of the cost while keeping the function forced
every component to justify itself — which is a much better simulation of real
engineering work than an unconstrained build would have been.
